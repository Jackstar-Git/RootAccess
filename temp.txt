In the past, an algorithm was a clear thing: a precise sequence of instructions that generates an output from an input (like a recipe for a cake, only the computer doesn't accidentally forget the baking powder).</p>\n<span class='line-break'></span>\n<p>A classic algorithm is <strong>logical</strong>, <strong>predictable</strong>, and above all, <strong>transparent</strong>. If the program crashes, you can usually tell exactly which line caused the error. But suddenly, any software capable of sorting data or recognizing a pattern is dubbed <strong>\"Artificial Intelligence\"</strong>. The difference, however, is fundamental.</p>\n<span class='line-break'></span>\n<p>Genuine modern AI, particularly in the field of machine learning, is not based on rigid rules but on <strong>statistical probabilities</strong>. But because <strong>\"AI-powered\"</strong> sounds so much more impressive in a quarterly report than <em>\"we conducted a statistical analysis\"</em>, the label is inflationarily stuck on everything that produces an output from more than two variables. This leads consumers to believe that their washing machine is making <strong>\"intelligent decisions\"</strong> when, in reality, it's just weighing the laundry (something it could do twenty years ago, by the way).</p>\n<h2>The Stochastic Parrots and Their Chatter</h2>\n<p>When we talk about ChatGPT or similar Large Language Models (LLMs), we need to lift the veil of magic that Silicon Valley has so carefully woven around these systems. An LLM is not a digital philosopher or an all-knowing god.</p>\n<span class='line-break'></span>\n<p>At its core, it's an extremely advanced version of the autocompletion on your smartphone, trained with the entire text mass of the internet. The functionality can be reduced to the principle of <strong>\"Next-Token-Prediction\"</strong>: The model mathematically calculates which word component (token) is most likely to follow next, based on the billions of sentences it has <em>\"read\"</em> during training.<span style=\"color:#ff6347\"><sup>2</sup></span> When you ask it how it's doing, it doesn't respond out of feeling but because the statistical probability for the answer sequence <em>\"I'm doing well, thank you for asking\"</em> is extremely high in its dataset.</p>\n<span class='line-break'></span>\n<p>Scientists have coined the apt term <strong>\"stochastic parrots\"</strong> for this phenomenon.<span style=\"color:#ff6347\"><sup>3</sup></span> These systems imitate language without ever understanding the meaning behind it. They're like a parrot living in a lecture hall for theoretical physics, eventually chattering along so perfectly that you might mistake it for a professor, until you ask it why it's speaking, and it responds with just a blank stare.</p>\n<h2>Why the Comparison with the Internet Falls Short</h2>\n<p>Often, the argument is made that with every new technology - be it the internet, smartphones, or calculators - we've heard the same doomsday chorus. <em>\"People will forget how to calculate,\"</em> they said about calculators. <em>\"The internet will kill libraries,\"</em> they said in the nineties. And yet, humanity has survived and adapted. But AI is not just a tool for accessing information like the internet.</p>\n<span class='line-break'></span>\n<p>The internet merely made access to knowledge easier; it was the infrastructure, a kind of digital postman. AI, on the other hand, is the first attempt to automate the cognitive processing of this knowledge. For the first time in history, we have a technology that not only supports human intelligence and behavior but widely imitates it. A calculator computes the square root of a number, but it doesn't write your term paper on the causes of the Thirty Years' War (and it doesn't claim with utter conviction that Napoleon was actually an avid surfer). The danger with AI is that we're not just delegating a task but handing over the process of thinking and judging itself to a black box whose decision-making bases we often don't understand even in the slightest.</p>\n<h2>When It Makes Sense: Tool Instead of Oracle</h2>\n<p>Despite all the justified skepticism and opinions of cynics (of which I am one), there are areas where the use of this technology is not only sensible but downright liberating.</p>\n<span class='line-break'></span>\n<p>The key is not to see AI as an oracle that knows the truth but as a kind of high-speed secretariat or inspiration engine. Anyone sitting in front of a blank document with no idea how to start can receive valuable impulses from an AI draft to overcome their writer's block.</p>\n<span class='line-break'></span>\n<p>The technology is particularly strong when we dictate the <strong>context to it completely</strong>. If I give the system my own notes, research, and facts and say: <strong>\"Structure this for me\"</strong>, then the AI functions as an excellent editor. In this closed system, the danger of hallucinations (i.e., the moment when the AI starts making things up with great confidence) decreases because it doesn't have to generate facts from <em>\"nothing\"</em> but works on the provided prompt.</p>\n<span class='line-break'></span>\n<p>It's like a hammer: you can build a house with it or hit your thumb. The problem is that currently, many people are trying to eat soup with a hammer. <em>(Or something like that)</em></p>\n<hr>\n<h2>The Creeping Atrophy of the Mind - Or: Why We're Unlearning How to Think</h2>\n<p>One of the most subtle but dangerous risks of current AI development is <strong>cognitive atrophy</strong>, a creeping muscle wasting of the mind. We've already seen a generation that can't find a supermarket two streets away without Google Maps because their sense of orientation has been outsourced to a blue dot on the screen.</p>\n<span class='line-break'></span>\n<p>With generative AI, we're taking this outsourcing process to the next level: we're now delegating the structuring of thoughts and the formulation of arguments, or even having them invented entirely. We must realize that writing and arguing aren't just about recording and presenting results. Writing is the process of thinking itself. While we painstakingly search for the right word or try to squeeze a complex issue into a logical sentence, we force our brain to perform at its best. We check contradictions, weigh information, and sharpen our own opinion. If we eliminate this <strong>\"cognitive resistance\"</strong> by asking AI to <em>\"quickly summarize something\"</em> or <em>\"formulate a convincing argument\"</em>, we rob ourselves of the exercise that makes us critical thinkers in the first place <em>(a toast to convenience, which makes us as intellectually flexible as a slice of toast bread in the long run)</em>.</p>\n<span class='line-break'></span>\n<p>This phenomenon is particularly evident in education and the workplace. When students have their essays written by AI, they don't just miss out on the text but also the intellectual engagement with the topic. They no longer learn how to synthesize information or develop their own voice. In the professional world, too, blind trust in AI output leads us to overlook nuances. Because the models formulate so eloquently and politely, we tend to question their output less critically than the text of a real person <em>(who might make spelling mistakes or seem unsympathetic)</em>.</p>\n<span class='line-break'></span>\n<p>This <strong>\"manipulation through eloquence\"</strong>, as I like to call it, is a real danger because the likelihood increases that we unconsciously adopt logical errors or factual nonsense simply because the tone sounds so authoritative and flawless. When the machine speaks with the authority of a professor, hardly anyone questions whether it's just calculating the statistical probability of nonsense. We risk becoming mere <strong>\"prompt pushers\"</strong> who know which button to press but have no idea what's happening in the machine room.</p>\n<h2>The Paradox of Emptiness</h2>\n<p>We're heading towards a scenario that I like to call the <strong>\"Great Irony Dilemma of Modernity\"</strong>. Imagine an everyday office life that is already partially a reality today: An employee uses AI to generate a detailed, five-page email from three bullet points because it looks <em>\"more professional\"</em> (and they don't feel like writing anyway). The recipient of this email gets this digital wall of text but, faced with their own flood of emails, has no time or desire to read it. So they also use AI to summarize the text back into three bullet points. In this cycle, an enormous amount of computing power and electricity has been consumed<span style=\"color:#ff6347\"><sup>4</sup></span>, only to end up with exactly the same information that was there at the beginning. The net gain in information is zero <em>(or even negative, as things tend to get lost in all this \"prompt pushing\")</em>, but both parties feel incredibly efficient and productive, even though they've only participated in an artificial inflation of reality.</p>\n<h2>The \"Dead Internet\": When Bots Write for Bots</h2>\n<p>Closely linked to this emptiness is the so-called <strong>\"Dead Internet Theory\"</strong>. What used to sound like a paranoid conspiracy theory now feels like a frighteningly accurate prediction. The theory states that the internet died years ago and is now almost exclusively inhabited by bots that create AI-generated content to influence other bots or manipulate search engine rankings. If you look at platforms like X or the comment sections of major news sites today, you increasingly encounter <strong>\"zombie content\"</strong>: interchangeable, soulless texts that only serve to place keywords. The problem is that AI models now start feeding on the data that other AIs have already published on the internet <em>(pardon the wording, but <strong>\"digital incest\"</strong> describes it almost better)</em>.</p>\n<span class='line-break'></span>\n<p>Scientists warn here of <strong>\"model collapse\"</strong>: when an AI is trained with AI-generated data, it starts to amplify errors and lose its own variance until the result is nothing but incomprehensible gibberish.<span style=\"color:#ff6347\"><sup>5</sup></span></p>\n<span class='line-break'></span>\n<p>We're flooding the net with SEO-optimized garbage written for algorithms, not humans. This makes it increasingly difficult to find genuine human experiences, original thoughts, or even reliable sources. The internet is transforming from a global knowledge repository into an echo chamber of insignificance, where AIs confirm each other's hallucinations while the human user frustratingly tries to find a single genuine experience report among three million AI-generated product reviews <em>(which was probably also written by a bot to cash in on commissions)</em>.</p>\n<h2>Censorship, Bias, and the Silicon Valley Filter</h2>\n<p>Another often underrated risk is the creeping influence on our societal opinion landscape through the training data of the models. An AI is never neutral - it's a reflection of the data it's been fed and the filters its creators have imposed on it. Since most large models are developed in Silicon Valley, they inevitably carry the cultural and moral values of that region. This leads to a kind of <strong>\"censorship of the average\"</strong>, where controversial or complex opinions are often suppressed in favor of a smoothed-over, politically correct, or simply Western perspective.</p>\n<span class='line-break'></span>\n<p>AI thus becomes a tool for subtle manipulation that doesn't work through prohibitions but through statistical weighting. Those who ask the model for historical or political assessments often get the <em>\"safest\"</em> answer, which can lead to a flattening of discourse in the long run. When the machine decides what's <em>\"appropriate\"</em>, we give up a piece of our cultural sovereignty to private tech companies.</p>\n<h2>The Democratization of Fraud</h2>\n<p>Finally, we must not forget the dark side of the coin: the massive increase in scams and spam. In the past, it was relatively easy to recognize a fraudulent email - poor grammar and absurd content were the warning signs. Thanks to AI, scammers can now compose perfect, personalized messages in any language in seconds, so convincing that even experienced users fall for them. We're being flooded with a torrent of generic content designed solely to steal attention or extract money. The <strong>\"dead internet\"</strong>, where bots have taken over, is no longer a dystopian fairy tale but a real threat to the integrity of our digital communication.</p>\n<hr>\n<h2>Conclusion: The Mouth Without Knowledge</h2>\n<p>In conclusion, we can certainly describe ChatGPT and its ilk as an eloquently chattering mouth without any real knowledge behind it. The danger lies less in the technology itself than in the enthusiastic volume of those who sell it as a panacea without understanding the statistical nature of these systems.</p>\n<span class='line-break'></span>\n<p>We should use AI for what it is: a fascinating, highly efficient tool for specific tasks that can take work off our hands but never our thinking. Those who rely on digital snake oil and believe they can solve all cognitive problems with it will find in the end that they've produced a lot of text, but the essence of understanding has been lost. <strong>Stay critical</strong>, question the <em>\"magical\"</em> promises, and remember that true intelligence is more than just predicting the next word.</p>\n<span class='line-break'></span>\n<p><span style=\"color:#ff6347\"><em>(Written with the quiet hope that no bot will use this text to generate a \"better\" version of it.)</em></span></p>\n<hr>\n<h3>Sources:</h3>\n<p><strong>Thumbnail: </strong> Adapted and modified from an image generated with Gemini (Nano Banana 2)</p>\n<p><strong>1)</strong> <a href=\"https://pharmaceutical-journal.com/article/opinion/the-history-of-snake-oil\">Haynes, A.
